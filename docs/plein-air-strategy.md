# Plein Air Painting — Strategy and Legal Framework

*Christopher Rehm — Bavaria*
*Last updated: April 2026*

---

## Purpose

Public plein air painting as a tool for building name recognition in the
local and regional art market. The goal is visibility and reputation first;
direct sales and gallery interest follow from that.

This document covers the strategy, the legal framework in Germany, and the
specific operational details for working in and around Augsburg, Landsberg
am Lech, and Munich.

---

## The Core Idea

Paint in public, in recognizable locations, on a regular schedule. Do not
attempt to sell on the spot. Funnel all interest to the website and the
gallery via a small sign and business cards.

This decouples three things that are usually tangled together:

1. **The act of painting** — protected, low-friction, no permit needed in most cases
2. **Visibility and reputation** — the actual goal
3. **The transaction** — handled separately, online or via the gallery

By keeping these separate, the legal complexity collapses to almost nothing,
and the activity becomes sustainable as a regular practice rather than a
one-off event requiring permits and paperwork.

## Legal Framework

### The General Principle

German street and public-space law distinguishes between two categories:

- **Gemeingebrauch** — general public use of streets and public spaces
  (walking, standing, looking, communicating). No permit required.
- **Sondernutzung** — special use beyond normal traffic purposes
  (selling goods, setting up stands, commercial activity). Requires a
  *Sondernutzungserlaubnis* from the local authority.

### Where Plein Air Painting Sits

Standing at an easel and painting is a gray area, but the gray favors the
artist. Key points:

- The Bundesverwaltungsgericht has ruled that street art is protected under
  *Kunstfreiheit* (Art. 5 Abs. 3 GG).
- For regular artistic activity, even where a permit is technically required,
  the authorities' discretion is reduced essentially to zero — they have to
  grant it.
- For spontaneous art, requiring a permit may itself be unconstitutional
  if it would make the activity practically impossible.
- Painting at an easel without blocking foot traffic, without a stand,
  and without selling, is very unlikely to attract any official attention.

### Where the Line Gets Crossed

The moment you do any of the following, the activity becomes
*Sondernutzung* and requires a permit:

- Setting up a stand or table to display works for sale
- Posting prices on works
- Initiating sales conversations on the spot
- Blocking foot traffic with equipment
- Painting directly on the street surface (this is explicitly prohibited
  in Munich and similar restrictions exist elsewhere)

### Munich is a Special Case

Munich's Altstadt-Fußgängerzone and several adjacent streets
(Schrammerstraße, Dienerstraße, Landschaftstraße, Sendlinger Straße, Tal)
have a formal permit system for any street art activity, including painting.

- Permits are issued by the Stadtinformation im Rathaus
- Cost: €10 per permit, administrative fee
- Process: First-time artists must present their work in person before
  receiving a permit. After that, permits can be reserved weekly.
- Daily allocation: Two permits per day for painters, with playing time
  from 12:15 to 22:00
- Alternative: A weekly block of up to 20 hours, used flexibly within
  the permitted time window
- Reservations open Thursday at 12:00 for the following week
- Phone: 089 / 233-92084

Painting directly on the street surface is not permitted under any
circumstances.

### Augsburg and Smaller Bavarian Cities

There is no centralized permit system for street art equivalent to Munich's.
The principle is the same — Sondernutzung requires permission — but in
practice, painting at an easel without selling is unlikely to be challenged.

**Recommended action:** A direct call to the Augsburg Ordnungsamt to ask
the following question, in German:

> "Brauche ich eine Genehmigung, wenn ich mit einer Staffelei auf öffentlichen
> Plätzen wie dem Rathausplatz male, ohne etwas zu verkaufen?"

This gives a definitive local answer and costs nothing. Do this once,
document the answer (ideally get an email), and the question is settled.

## Operational Setup

### What to Bring

- Easel and painting kit (the actual tools of the trade)
- A small, tasteful sign — not a sales pitch. Brass-plate aesthetic, not
  market-stall. Contains:
  - Name
  - Website URL: artbychristopherrehm.com
  - Instagram handle: @christopher.rehm.14
  - QR code linking to the website
- Business cards in a small holder
- Nothing that looks like a stand, no prices, no "for sale" signage

### How to Handle Interactions

When someone asks about buying:

> "Thank you. Everything I make is available through my website or through
> Gallery Auxburg in Augsburg. Here is my card."

This is honest, clean, and keeps the activity firmly on the visibility side
of the line. It also funnels interest into channels where the actual sale
can happen properly — with payment, paperwork, and (where the gallery is
involved) the relationship-building that comes with that.

### Frequency and Consistency

Once or twice a week during good weather, ideally on a regular schedule
(e.g. Wednesday afternoons in Augsburg, Saturday mornings in Landsberg).

Consistency matters more than volume. The goal is for the same regular
foot-traffic — locals walking to work, retirees on their daily routes,
gallery owners going to lunch — to start recognizing the same artist in
the same places. That is how local name recognition actually builds.

A burst of activity followed by silence does not build reputation. A
steady, predictable presence does.

## Locations

### Augsburg

| Location | Why | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Rathausplatz | High foot traffic, iconic backdrop | Central, very visible |
| Maximilianstraße | Tourist + local mix, architectural views | Wide street, easy to set up without blocking |
| Lech canals (Altstadt) | Beautiful watercolour subjects | Quieter, better for actual painting focus |
| Near Schaezlerpalais | Art-oriented foot traffic | People going to/from museums are pre-qualified |
| Fuggerei area | Historic, photogenic | Strong Bavarian-character subject matter |

### Landsberg am Lech

| Location | Why | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hauptplatz | Iconic Bavarian square, the central image of the town | Highest visibility |
| Lech weir | Strong watercolour subject (water, architecture) | Quieter, good for serious work |
| Views from across the river | Best Altstadt panorama | Tourist-favored vantage points |

### Countryside (Lechfeld and Approach to the Alps)

Lower foot traffic, but very strong for content generation and for work
that connects to the British watercolour landscape tradition. Less useful
for face-to-face name building, more useful for the work itself and for
Instagram content.

### Munich (If Pursuing the Permit System)

| Location | Why | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Marienplatz area | Highest foot traffic in southern Germany | Permit required, but the audience density is enormous |
| Viktualienmarkt periphery | Art-buying demographic | Check permit boundaries — the market itself has its own rules |
| Sendlinger Tor area | Less crowded, still central | Within the permit zone |

## What This is Not

This strategy is deliberately *not* about street selling. It is not about
turning the act of painting into a transaction. It is about being visible,
being present, and being recognized as a working artist in a specific place
over time.

If the goal were direct on-the-spot sales, the permit and stand-allocation
system would be the right path — and that is what the markets document
covers separately. Plein air in public is a different tool with a different
purpose: building the reputation that makes everything else easier.
